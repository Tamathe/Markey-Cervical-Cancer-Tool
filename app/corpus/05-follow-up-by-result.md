title: What follow-up is recommended for each result
origin: ASCCP 2024 Enduring Consensus Guidelines for Cervical Cancer Screening and Management (updated from 2019)
url: https://www.asccp.org/enduring-guidelines
---

The follow-up step depends on **both** your Pap result and your HPV result together. These are simplified general guidelines. Your own doctor's recommendation may be different based on your age, your past results, or other things in your history.

**Normal Pap, HPV negative**: Repeat screening in **5 years**.

**Normal Pap, HPV positive — type 16 or 18**: **Colposcopy** is recommended. These two HPV types carry the most risk over time, so doctors look closer right away.

**Normal Pap, HPV positive — other high-risk types**: A repeat test in **1 year** is usually recommended, or a newer triage test (see below). Most of these infections clear on their own in a year. No procedure is needed now.

**ASC-US, HPV negative**: Repeat screening in **3 years**. Lower risk because the virus is not there.

**ASC-US, HPV positive**: **Colposcopy** is recommended for a closer look at the cervix.

**LSIL, HPV positive** (or HPV unknown): **Colposcopy** is recommended.

**LSIL, HPV negative**: Repeat Pap and HPV test in 1 year, **or** colposcopy — your doctor will discuss which is right for you.

**ASC-H**: **Colposcopy** is recommended. The cells could be more significant.

**HSIL**: **Colposcopy** and treatment to remove the abnormal area. This is to prevent cancer years down the road. For people over 25 with HSIL, some doctors may recommend treatment at the same visit as the colposcopy — this is called **expedited treatment**.

**AGC, AIS, or higher**: **Colposcopy** with extra sampling (endocervical and sometimes endometrial). Treatment depends on what is found.

**Unsatisfactory** (sample was not good): Repeat the test in **2 to 4 months**.

**About the HPV type number.** Newer HPV tests can report which specific type of HPV you have. HPV 16 and HPV 18 cause the most cervical cancers, so a positive result for those two types leads to colposcopy sooner. Positive results for other high-risk types (like 31, 33, 45, 52, 58) are taken seriously but usually get a repeat test or a triage test first. If your result lists a number, that is why.

**About dual stain (p16/Ki-67).** Some labs now offer a follow-up test called **dual stain** for people who are HPV positive with a normal Pap. It looks for two proteins that show up together when HPV is actually starting to change cells. If the dual stain is negative, repeat testing in 1 year may be enough. If it is positive, colposcopy is recommended. This test can save some people from a colposcopy they did not need.

If you are pregnant, the follow-up plan may be different — colposcopy is safe in pregnancy, but most treatments are delayed until after delivery.
