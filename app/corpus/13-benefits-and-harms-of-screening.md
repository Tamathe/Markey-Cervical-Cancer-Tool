Cervical cancer screening works. Very few people who get screened on schedule develop cervical cancer. When screening finds a change early, treatment can stop cancer before it ever starts.

**Why screening is so effective.**
Cervical cancer is one of the slowest cancers to develop. A cell change usually takes **10 to 20 years** to become cancer, if it ever does. That is a long window for a Pap or HPV test to catch it and treat it.

**False positive results.**
Sometimes a screening test looks abnormal even though there is no real problem. This is called a **false positive**. It can lead to extra tests like colposcopy, and to worry. The follow-up tests are safe, but they take time and can be uncomfortable.

**False negative results.**
Sometimes a screening test looks normal even when there is a change. This is called a **false negative**. It is uncommon, but it is one reason screening is repeated every few years instead of just once.

**Finding things that would not have caused harm.**
Some cell changes would have gone away on their own, but we cannot always tell which ones. Treating a change that would have cleared on its own is called **overtreatment**. This is why low-grade changes (LSIL, ASC-US) are often watched instead of treated right away.

**Treatment can affect future pregnancy.**
Treatments that remove tissue from the cervix (called LEEP or cone biopsy) can slightly raise the chance of early labor in a future pregnancy. This is one reason doctors are careful not to treat changes that are likely to clear on their own.

**Numbers that may help.**
For every 1,000 people screened over many years:
- About **1 cervical cancer death is prevented**.
- About **150 will have an abnormal result** that needs follow-up.
- About **80 will have a colposcopy**.
- About **50 will have some treatment** of the cervix.

Most of those abnormal results and follow-ups turn out fine. The point of screening is to find the rare serious change early enough to stop it.

**Screening is less effective for some people.**
- People with **obesity** can be harder to screen well because the cervix can be harder to see.
- People with **HIV** or a **weakened immune system** need to be screened more often.
- People who were exposed to **DES** (a drug given to some pregnant women decades ago) need different screening.

If any of these apply to you, your doctor will adjust the plan.
