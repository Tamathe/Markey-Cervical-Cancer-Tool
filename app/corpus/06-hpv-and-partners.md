title: HPV, sex, and partners
origin: CDC — HPV Frequently Asked Questions

HPV is the most common sexually transmitted infection. About **8 out of 10** sexually active people get it at some point. Most people never know they had it, because the body usually clears it on its own.

**When did I get it?** Often it is impossible to know. HPV can stay in the body without causing any changes for many years before it ever shows up on a test. You may have had it for a long time before this test. A new positive HPV test does **not** mean a partner was unfaithful.

**Does my partner need to be tested?** There is **no reliable HPV test for men** in routine care. Men are usually not tested. There is no treatment for HPV itself — only for the cell changes it can cause. So testing a partner usually does not change what anyone does.

**Do I have to tell my partner?** It is your choice. HPV is not the same as some other sexually transmitted infections where notification is required by law. Many people choose to share because it can help a partner think about their own screening or vaccination.

**Can my partner give it back to me?** If your partner has the same strain, they likely already had it before — sharing is so common that re-infection from the same partner is not usually a concern. If you are starting a new relationship, condoms lower the risk of HPV but do not prevent it completely (HPV is on the skin, not just in body fluids).

**Can I still have sex?** Yes. HPV does not require you to stop having sex. Talk to your doctor if you are not sure.

**Can my partner get the HPV vaccine?** Yes. The HPV vaccine is approved for males and females ages 9 to 26 (and through age 45 with a doctor's recommendation). It works best **before** any sexual activity, but it can still help.

HPV is common, manageable, and rarely cancer. Cell changes — when they happen — can be caught early and treated.
