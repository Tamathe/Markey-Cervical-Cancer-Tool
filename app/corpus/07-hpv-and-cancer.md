title: HPV and cancer — what the risk really is
origin: National Cancer Institute — HPV and Cancer
---

Most HPV does **not** cause cancer. The body clears most HPV infections within 1 to 2 years without treatment.

A small number of HPV infections do not clear. When the same high-risk type of HPV stays in the body for **many years**, it can slowly change the cells of the cervix. Those cell changes are what a Pap test looks for. If cell changes are found, they can be treated long before they ever become cancer.

How long does it take? Cervical cancer typically takes **10 to 20 years** to develop from a long-lasting HPV infection. That is why regular screening works so well — there is plenty of time to find and treat changes early.

**Cervical cancer is almost 100% treatable when found early.** Most cases today are in people who were not screened regularly.

HPV can also cause other cancers: anal, oropharyngeal (throat), vulvar, vaginal, and penile. Cervical cancer is the most common HPV-related cancer.

If your test is HPV positive, it means the virus is there. It does **not** mean you have cancer. It does **not** mean you will get cancer. It means you and your doctor will follow up at the right time so any cell changes can be caught early — long before they would ever be dangerous.

The vaccine prevents new HPV infections from the most cancer-causing types. It does not treat HPV you already have, but it can still protect against the strains you have not yet been exposed to.
