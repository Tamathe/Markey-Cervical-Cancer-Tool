title: What happens after a colposcopy — treatment options
origin: ACOG FAQ 217 — Treatment for Cervical Pre-cancer / ASCCP 2019
---

After a colposcopy, the doctor will tell you what the biopsy showed. The biopsy is graded as **CIN 1, CIN 2, or CIN 3** (CIN stands for "cervical intraepithelial neoplasia" — pre-cancer cell changes).

**CIN 1 (mild change):** Most CIN 1 changes clear on their own. Often, the recommendation is to watch and repeat testing in a year, not to treat.

**CIN 2 or CIN 3 (moderate to severe change):** Treatment is usually recommended to remove the abnormal area. Treatment prevents these changes from becoming cancer years down the road.

Common treatments:

**LEEP (Loop Electrosurgical Excision Procedure).** A thin wire loop with a small electrical current is used to gently remove the abnormal area of the cervix. Done in clinic, usually under local numbing. Takes 5 to 10 minutes. You go home the same day.

**Cold-knife cone biopsy.** A larger piece of the cervix is removed, usually in an operating room. Used when the area is large or hard to reach. Done under anesthesia.

**Cryotherapy.** Freezes the abnormal cells. Done in clinic, no cutting.

**Laser ablation.** Uses a laser to destroy the abnormal cells. Done in clinic.

After treatment:

- Spotting and watery discharge are normal for 2 to 4 weeks.
- No tampons, no douching, no vaginal sex for about 4 weeks, or as your doctor tells you.
- Cramping the first day or two is normal — over-the-counter pain medicine helps.
- You will need close follow-up testing for the next few years to make sure the changes do not come back.

**Will treatment affect future pregnancy?** For most people, no. LEEP and similar treatments do slightly raise the chance of preterm birth, so the doctor will weigh this if you might want to be pregnant later. They will choose the treatment that protects future fertility as much as possible.

Treatment of pre-cancer changes is **very effective** — most people are cured with one procedure and routine follow-up.
