Pap results use short words and letters that can look scary. Here is what they mean in plain language. Most of these are **not cancer**. Most of the time they mean "we need a closer look" or "let's check again later."

**Negative** or **Normal**: No abnormal cells were found. Nothing to do right now.

**ASC-US** (Atypical Squamous Cells of Undetermined Significance): The cells look slightly different from normal, but the lab isn't sure why. It is the most common abnormal Pap result. It is often caused by HPV or by other things like irritation. It is not cancer.

**LSIL** (Low-grade Squamous Intraepithelial Lesion): A small, low-grade cell change, usually caused by HPV. Most LSIL changes clear on their own. This is not cancer.

**HSIL** (High-grade Squamous Intraepithelial Lesion): A more significant cell change. Still not cancer, but it is the kind of change that, if left alone for many years, could become cancer. It is treated to remove the abnormal cells.

**ASC-H** (Atypical Squamous Cells, cannot exclude HSIL): The cells could be more serious. A colposcopy is recommended.

**AGC** (Atypical Glandular Cells): A change in the gland cells of the cervix. Less common; needs a closer look.

**AIS** (Adenocarcinoma in situ): An early, pre-cancer change in gland cells. Treatable.

**Unsatisfactory**: The sample was not good enough for the lab to read (often just not enough cells). The test needs to be repeated in 2 to 4 months. It does **not** mean anything is wrong.

**HPV positive**: The HPV test found high-risk HPV in your cells. By itself, this is not cancer and not even cell changes — it just means the virus is there.

**HPV negative**: No high-risk HPV was found. This is reassuring.

**HPV 16 or HPV 18 positive**: Some newer HPV tests can tell which type. HPV 16 and 18 are the two types most likely to cause cervical cancer over many years. A positive result for these is taken seriously sooner — usually a colposcopy is recommended. It is still not cancer.

**HPV positive — other high-risk types** (sometimes listed as 31, 33, 45, 52, 58, or "other high-risk"): Less risky than 16 or 18 in the short term. Usually a repeat test in 1 year is recommended. Most of these infections clear on their own.

**Dual stain positive** or **p16/Ki-67 positive**: A newer follow-up test. A positive result means the HPV is starting to affect the cells. A colposcopy is usually recommended. A negative dual stain means the HPV is there but is not changing cells right now.
